What Happens to Your TSP When You Leave Federal Service

Your Thrift Savings Plan doesn't disappear when you separate — but the decisions you make in the first 90 days can cost or save you tens of thousands over a career. Here are your four options and how to think about them.

Option 1: Leave it in the TSP

If your balance is $200 or more, you can leave everything right where it is. Your money keeps growing, you can still rebalance among the funds, and you keep some of the lowest expense ratios in the retirement industry. What changes: you can no longer contribute, and any outstanding TSP loan must be resolved (repay it or it becomes a taxable distribution).

For many separated feds this is the right default while you sort out the bigger career questions. There is no deadline forcing you to move.

Option 2: Roll it into an IRA

A direct rollover to a traditional IRA keeps the money tax-deferred and opens up unlimited investment choices. Roth TSP balances roll to a Roth IRA. The trade-offs: most IRA funds cost more than TSP funds, and IRAs have weaker protection from creditors in some states.

One trap worth knowing: if you separate in or after the year you turn 55, you can take TSP withdrawals without the 10% early-withdrawal penalty. Roll to an IRA and that age drops to 59½ — you give the exception up. Special-category employees (law enforcement, firefighters) get the exception at 50.

Option 3: Transfer to your new employer's 401(k)

If your next employer's plan accepts roll-ins, consolidating can simplify your life and preserves the age-55 rule under that plan's terms. Compare fund expenses first — many corporate 401(k)s charge 5–10x what the TSP does.

Option 4: Cash out (almost always the wrong move)

A full withdrawal triggers ordinary income tax on the entire traditional balance, plus a 10% penalty if you're under the age exceptions. A $100,000 cash-out can shrink to $60,000–$65,000 after taxes and penalties — and you lose decades of compounding. If severance has you cash-strapped, exhaust other options first.

The short version

  • ✓Do a DIRECT rollover (custodian to custodian) — never take a check made out to you, which triggers 20% mandatory withholding.
  • ✓Do resolve any TSP loan before or at separation to avoid a surprise taxable distribution.
  • ✓Do compare expense ratios before moving money out of the TSP — cheap matters over 20 years.
  • ✗Don't cash out to bridge a job gap until you've exhausted severance, unemployment, and taxable savings.
  • ✗Don't roll to an IRA at 55–59½ without understanding you're giving up penalty-free access.
  • ✗Don't make this decision in week one — nothing expires.
Educational, not advice: This guide explains general rules that change and have exceptions. Verify current figures with OPM, TSP.gov, and IRS guidance, and consult a licensed professional about your specific situation before acting.
